Geometric mean

The geometric mean (GM) of a set of values x1,x2,…,xn{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\)"} is as follows:

GM(x)=(∏i=1nxi)1/n=(x1x2⋯xn)1/n.{"version":"1.1","math":"GM(\mathbf{x}) = \left(\prod_{i=1}^n x_i \right)^{1/n} = (x_1x_2\cdots x_n)^{1/n}. "}

Let’s say we have a set of items which are evaluated on different merits, all with different scales. The geometric mean allows us to obtain a meaningful ‘average’ to compare the overall value of an item. It effectively ‘normalises’ the ranges being averaged so that no one value/metric dominates the others due to their range.

To understand the geometric mean and where it should be used over the arithmetic mean, consider the following scenario.

Your salary goes up by 20%{"version":"1.1","math":"\(20\%\)"} one year and 10%{"version":"1.1","math":"\(10\%\)"} the next. What is the average salary increase per year?

The first guess would be to say 15% per year (the arithmetic mean of the percentages), which is incorrect. Let us verify this with a few calculations and assuming an initial salary:
