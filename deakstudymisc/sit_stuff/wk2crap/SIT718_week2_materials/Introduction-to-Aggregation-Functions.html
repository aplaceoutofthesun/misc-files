Introduction to Aggregation Functions

Aggregation functions allow us to process a range of data and use a single value to represent or describe some feature of the data.

The converse is not necessarily true i.e. functions that return a single value that describes some feature of the data are not necessarily classified as aggregation functions.

This week we will explore examples of both, implement them in R, and assess their properties and implications.

MAIA ANGELOVA TURKEDJIEVA: Data-- and in particular, big data-- can be overwhelming. We might be able to make sense of a small set of numbers, like 10 or so. But beyond these, normally, one needs some assistance from computer technology. When making decisions about existing data, we also often rely on past experience and a sense of what is expected with respect to each of the potential outcomes. But our sense can often mislead us, even if we are very experienced. In order to assist our decision making, we use aggregation functions. They are mathematical ways to summarise and aggregate even large set of data. Examples of simple aggregation functions are the arithmetic mean, the harmonic mean, and the geometric mean.

Let's explore the potentials of these functions to inform us about the data. We will introduce more complex functions of many variables, attributes, or properties of the data to assist you further. You will explore how some of these functions are used in economics, ecology, and biology. You will also practise some of those handy R skills to compute various means and medians with examples from real-world data.
