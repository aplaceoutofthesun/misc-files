Harmonic Mean

The harmonic mean (or subcontrary mean) is most useful for calculating the average of rates or speeds. Consider the following example:

Vicky can paint a house in 3 hours and Simon can paint a house in 5 hours. How long would it take them to paint two houses if they work together? Assume that they both are painting at the same time on either house with no breaks.

4 hours (the arithmetic meanof 3 and 5) would be incorrect as after 4 hours, Vicky would have painted 43{"version":"1.1","math":"\(4 \over 3\)"} houses, and Simon would have painted 45{"version":"1.1","math":"\(4 \over 5\) "} houses. Combined together they would have pained 43+45=3215≈2.13{"version":"1.1","math":"\(\frac{4}{3} + \frac{4}{5} = \frac{32}{15} \approx 2.13\)"} houses which is more than two.

8 hours (3 hourse + 5 hours) would only be correct if Vicky painted her house first, then Simon painted his house (or vice versa). If they both were working at the same time then Vicky would have painted more than two houses by herself in 8 hours.

However, if we use the harmonic mean, we obtain a better estimate.

Given a set of values x1,x2,…,xn{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\)"}, the harmonic mean is calculated as follows:

HM(x)=n(∑i=1n1x1)−1=n1x1+1x2+⋯+1xn{"version":"1.1","math":"HM(x) = n \left(\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{x_1} \right)^{-1} = \dfrac{n}{\frac{1}{x_1} + \frac{1}{x_2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{x_n}} "}

As a simple example, the harmonic mean of 7, 1 and 8 is

HM(7,1,8)=(317+11+18)=16871≈2.37.{"version":"1.1","math":"HM(7, 1, 8) = \left(\frac{3}{\frac{1}{7} + \frac{1}{1} + \frac{1}{8}}\right) = \frac{168}{71} \approx 2.37. "}

Going back to our house-painting example, the harmonic mean can be applied as follows, using a rate of 3 hours per house, and 5 hours per house for Vicky and Simon respectively.

HM(3,5)=2×(13+15)−1=308=3.75{"version":"1.1","math":"HM(3,5) = 2 \times \left(\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{5}\right)^{-1} = \frac{30}{8} = 3.75 "}

    • Since each value is put as the denominator of a fraction, having a zero as one of our values would result in the fraction being undefined, leading to an inconclusive calculation of the harmonic mean.

    As a result, the harmonic mean is defined to be zero whenever any of its inputs are zero.       • The R code for the harmonic mean is as follows:
      HM = function(x) {
  if(prod(x)==0) {
    return (0)
  } else {
    length(x)/sum(1/x)
  }
}
