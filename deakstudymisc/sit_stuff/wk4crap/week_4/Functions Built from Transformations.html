Functions Built from Transformations

We can use transformations to build new functions.

One such type of function is called the dual aggregation function, which is built from a negation.

  • For a given aggregation function A{"version":"1.1","math":"\(A\)"}, it’s dual Ad{"version":"1.1","math":"\(A^d\)"}  is given by:
Ad(x)=1−A(1−x1,1−x2,…,1−xn){"version":"1.1","math":"\\A^d(\mathbf x) = 1 - A(1-x_1,1-x_2, \ldots, 1-x_n)"}
  • Let’s see what happens when we take the dual of some of our important functions.

The geometric mean and its dual GMd{"version":"1.1","math":"\(GM^d\)"}.


 Geometric mean and its dual GMd{"version":"1.1","math":"\(GM^d\)"} 1


 Geometric mean and its dual GMd{"version":"1.1","math":"\(GM^d\)"} 2
  • The dual function essentially exhibits reciprocal properties to the original function, but it remains monotone increasing.
  • We note that the geometric mean of any set which includes xi=0{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x_i = 0\)"} will have an output of zero.
  • With its dual, any input set which includes  x1=0{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x_1=0\)"} will automatically have an output of 1.

The arithmetic mean and its dual AMd{"version":"1.1","math":"\(AM^d\)"}.


 Arithmetic mean and its dual AMd{"version":"1.1","math":"\(AM^d\)"}

Since the arithmetic mean exhibits uniform behaviour over the interval, its dual is actually same function.
