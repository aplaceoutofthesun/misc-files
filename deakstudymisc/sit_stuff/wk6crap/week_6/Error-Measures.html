Error Measures

We can measure a model’s accuracy by comparing expected outputs with actual outputs in a few different ways.

We’ll briefly mention a few error measures and how to calculate them, but not much additional analysis beyond that. For notation:

  • xi{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x_i\)"} are our input values,
  • Aw(xi){"version":"1.1","math":"\(A_w(x_i)\)"}  is our expected output values based on the model,
  • yi{"version":"1.1","math":"\(y_i\)"} are our observed output values.

Total Squared Error/Sum of Squared Error (SSE)

This can be described as all of the differences between the predicted and observed output values, squared and then added together.

RMSE=∑i=1n(Aw(xi)−yi)2n{"version":"1.1","math":"\\ RMSE=\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n}\frac{(A_w(x_i)-y_i)^2}{n}} \\"}

  • The RMSE can be interpreted as the average difference between each prediction and the output.
  • It is also the quadratic mean, a power mean with p=2{"version":"1.1","math":"\(p=2\)"} ,  
    • so it will be affected more by larger differences.
    • If a fitted function performs better than another in terms of RMSE, then it will also have a lower total squared error.

    Total Least Absolute Deviation (LAD) / Sum of Absolute Errors (SAE)

    The sum of all the absolute differences between predicted and observed outputs,  

SAE=∑i=1m|Aw(xi)−yi|{"version":"1.1","math":"SAE=\sum_{i=1}^{m}|A_w(x_i)-y_i|"}

Average L1 error / Average Absolute Error

This is the SAE divided by the number of observations,

Av.AE=∑i=1n|Aw(xi)−yi|n{"version":"1.1","math":"Av.AE=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\frac{|A_w(x_i)-y_i|}{n}"}

Pearson Correlation r{"version":"1.1","math":"\(r\)"}

The Pearson Correlation returns a value between −1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(-1\)"} and 1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(1\)"} that indicates how much of a linear relationship there is between two variables, where

  • r=−1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(r=-1\)"} indicates a perfect negative relationship,
  • r=0{"version":"1.1","math":"\(r=0\)"} indicates no relationship at all,
  • r=1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(r=1\)"} indicates a perfect positive relationship.

In terms of error analysis we want the predicted and observed outputs to be as close to each other as possible, which means that we’re looking for our Pearson Correlation to be close to 1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(1\)"}. We would not expect to find a negative relationship (r<0){"version":"1.1","math":"\(% <![CDATA[ (r < 0 )%]>\)"} at all.

Spearman Correlation (ρ{"version":"1.1","math":"\(\rho\)"}- pronounced 'rho')

The Spearman Correlation is similar to the Pearson Correlation but assesses monotonic relationships instead of linear relationships. In other words, it looks at whether the ranking/ordering of values relative to each other are consistent between the two variables.

  • p=−1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(p=-1\)"} indicates a monotonic decreasing relationship between the two variables.
  • p=0{"version":"1.1","math":"\(p=0\)"} indicates a lack of montonicity between the two variables (i.e. a line of best fit has a few twists and turns).
  • p=1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(p=1\)"} indicates a monotonic increasing relationship between the two variables.

For example, if our observed outputs were (0.5,0.95,0.3,0.72){"version":"1.1","math":"\((0.5, 0.95, 0.3, 0.72)\)"} and our predicted outputs were (0.7,0.89,0.66,0.72){"version":"1.1","math":"\((0.7, 0.89, 0.66, 0.72)\)"}, then ρ=1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(\rho=1\)"} 

since the relative rankings/orderings between the two sets are the same.

There are two interesting articles discussing the difference between Pearson Correlation and Spearman Correlation: A comparison of the Pearson and Spearman correlation methods and Correlation Coefficients: Appropriate Use and Interpretation.
