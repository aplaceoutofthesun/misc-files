Sketching Constraints

The easiest way to draw straight lines on a graph is to find any two points on the line, plot them on the graph and then draw the line through them.

The easiest two points to find are the x{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x\)"}-intercept and the y{"version":"1.1","math":"\(y\)"} -intercept i.e. the x{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x\)"}-coordinate at y=0{"version":"1.1","math":"\(y=0\)"} and the y{"version":"1.1","math":"\(y\)"}-coordinate at x{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x\)"} respectively.

Example:

2x+y≤100{"version":"1.1","math":"\(2x+y \leq 100\)"} we want to first draw the line 2x+y=100{"version":"1.1","math":"\(2x+y = 100\)"} .  We find our two points as follows:

  • Setting x=0{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x=0\)"} gives us y=100{"version":"1.1","math":"\(y=100\)"}, and therefore our first coordinate is (0,100){"version":"1.1","math":"\((0,100)\)"}.
  • Setting y=0{"version":"1.1","math":"\(y=0\)"} gives us2x=100{"version":"1.1","math":"\(2x = 100\)"}, giving us x=50{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x=50\)"} and therefore our second coordinate is (50,0){"version":"1.1","math":"\((50,0)\)"}.

To determine which side of the line to shade (to represent the inequality), choose a random point on one side and see if it satisfies the constraint. If it does, we can shade the side containing the point, and it not, we shade the other side.

Example:

With 2x+y≤100{"version":"1.1","math":"\(2x+y \leq 100\)"}, let’s test the point (0,0){"version":"1.1","math":"\((0,0)\)"}. Substituting (x,y)=(0,0){"version":"1.1","math":"\((x,y) = (0,0)\)"} into our inequality gives us 2×0+0≤100{"version":"1.1","math":"\(2\times 0 + 0 \leq 100\)"} which is satisfied, so therefore we can shade the bottom left side of the line (making sure to keep within the axes!).

After we do this for all constraints, the area where all the shading overlaps is called a feasible region. This is where we’ll look for our optimal solution using the objective function.

Types of Solutions

feasible solution

Is one that satisfies all constraints. For example, the point x=0,y=0{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x = 0, y = 0\)"} is a feasible solution although not the best solution.

The collection of points in the graph that make up a feasible solution is called the feasible region.

infeasible solution

Is one that violates one or more of the constraints. For example, x=50,y=50{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x = 50, y = 50\)"} is an infeasible solution since it violates the constraint 2x+y≤100{"version":"1.1","math":"\(2x+y \leq 100\)"}.

A optimal solution is a feasible solution that satisfies the objective function (which in this case, is maximising the profit).

It is possible to have more than one optimal solution and we will look at cases where this occurs.

Finding the Optimal Solution

Finding the optimal solution visually requires sketching our objective function and moving it up and down the graph until we reach the edge of the feasible region.

We start with a value estimate for z{"version":"1.1","math":"\(z\)"} (e.g. z=50{"version":"1.1","math":"\(z=50\)"}.  

and sketch our first isocost/isoprofit line. The goal is now to maintain the slope of this line (e.g. with a ruler) and since our objective is to maximise our profit, we’ll want to move our objective function up until we reach a corner or an edge of the feasible region.

Types of Solutions

Using the graphical method, there are four different scenarios that can happen:

  1. Single feasible solution - As we saw in our Toy Company example, the objective function stops at a single corner of the feasible region, giving a single optimal solution.
  2. No solution - LPs without a feasible region will have no solution.
  3. Infinite Solutions - This happens when the objective function lines up with an edge on the feasible region. This means that any point along that edge is also an optimal solution.
  4. Unbounded Feasible Region - There also exist LPs with feasible regions that are not enclosed. With a maximisation function, this effectively means that there is no optimal solution since we can always find a bigger one.
