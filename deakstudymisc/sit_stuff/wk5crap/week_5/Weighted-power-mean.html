For means other than the arithmetic mean, we need to be careful in interpreting weights and predicting how the function behaves.

Example

Suppose we have a geometric mean with w=(0.5,0.2,0.3){"version":"1.1","math":"\(\mathbf w = (0.5,0.2,0.3)\)"} and let x=(0.7,0.6,0.3){"version":"1.1","math":"\(\mathbf x = (0.7,0.6,0.3)\)"}. Then, 

GMw(x)=0.70.5×0.60.2×0.30.3=0.5264.{"version":"1.1","math":"\\ GM_{\mathbf w}(\mathbf x) = 0.7^{0.5} \times 0.6^{0.2} \times 0.3^{0.3} = 0.5264. \\"}

Now, if we increase x1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x_1\)"} by 0.1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(0.1\)"}, we have GMw(0.8,0.6,0.3)≈0.5627{"version":"1.1","math":"\(GM_{\mathbf w}(0.8,0.6,0.3) \approx 0.5627\)"}, however if we increase x3{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x_3\)"}  by 0.1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(0.1\)"}, we get GMw(0.7,0.6,0.4)≈0.5738{"version":"1.1","math":"\(GM_{\mathbf w}(0.7,0.6,0.4) \approx 0.5738\)"}. So we actually are better off increasing x3{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x_3\)"}, even though x1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x_1\)"} has the highest weight and considered “most important”.

Since the geometric mean is a variant of the power mean with p=0{"version":"1.1","math":"\(p=0\)"}, it is more affected by low inputs. In general, the weights still reflect the importance of each variable overall, but we also need to take into account the function’s behaviour.
