Other Weighting Conventions

We’ve shown you some ways weights can be shuffled around to account for some scenarios.

Here are a few more for some light reading!

Simpson’s Dominance Index

The concept of dominance (in economics and ecology) is a concept that can also be considered in the framework of weighted means. We let, qi{"version":"1.1","math":"\(q_i\)"} represent the proportional values, based on x{"version":"1.1","math":"\(\mathbf x\)"}, i.e. q=xi∑i=1nxi{"version":"1.1","math":"\(q = \frac{x_i}{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i}\)"}.

For example, if there are four species of birds and their populations in a particular region are given by x=(123,256,309,777){"version":"1.1","math":"\(\mathbf{x} = (123, 256, 309, 777)\)"},  then as proportions of the total number of birds we would have q=(0.084,0.175,0.211,0.530){"version":"1.1","math":"\(\mathbf{q} = (0.084, 0.175, 0.211, 0.530)\)"}.

Simpson’s Dominance Index calculates the degree to which a single species dominates the population, and is given by:

Simp(q)=∑i=1nqi2{"version":"1.1","math":"\\ Simp(\mathbf q) = \sum\limits_{i=1}^n {q_i^2} \\"}

We could also write this as WAMw(q){"version":"1.1","math":"\(WAM_{\mathbf w}(\mathbf q)\)"} where wi=qi{"version":"1.1","math":"\(w_i = q_i\)"} i.e. the weight depends on the population.

This function reaches a minimum when all of the inputs are equal, and reaches a maximum of 1 if one of the qi=1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(\mathbf q_i = 1\)"},  i.e. there is only one species (since this qi=1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(q_i=1\)"} means that xi{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x_i\)"} is 100%{"version":"1.1","math":"\(100\%\)"} of the total.

Entropy

Entropy can be thought of in a similar way, although it is not an aggregation function. it is expressed:

Entropy(q)=−∑i=1nqiln⁡qi{"version":"1.1","math":"\\ \mathrm{Entropy}(\mathbf q) = - \sum\limits_{i=1}^n q_i \ln q_i \\"}

which is similar to the geometric mean however without the inverse performed at the end.

The key difference with these kinds of weighting is that the effective weights will change depending on the inputs, i.e, the smaller the input relative to the remaining inputs, the smaller the weight that will be applied.

This type of ‘weighting convention’ does result in non-monotone behaviour, and so for the moment we will usually restrict ourself to functions where the weights do not change.
