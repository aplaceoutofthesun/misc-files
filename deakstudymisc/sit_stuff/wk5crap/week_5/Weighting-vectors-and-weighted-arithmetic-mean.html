Weighting Vectors & Weighted Arithmetic Mean

In the same way that we treat data as multiple vectors/arrays of values, we can treat weights in a similar manner.

The vector of parameters which we denote as w=(w1,w2,…,wn){"version":"1.1","math":"\(\mathbf w = (w_1, w_2, \dots, w_n)\)"} is often referred to as a weighting vector where the i th{"version":"1.1","math":"\(i^{\ th}\)"} wight usually corresponds with the i th{"version":"1.1","math":"\(i^{\ th}\)"} input. To keep them in line with aggregation functions, the weights should satisfy the following conditions:

  • all weights must be greater than or equal to zero i.e. wi≥0 for all i{"version":"1.1","math":"\(w_i \geq 0 \text{ for all } i\)"}
  • adding up all weights must equal 1 i.e. ∑i=1nwi=1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(\sum_{i=1}^n w_i = 1\)"}  For the purposes of aggregation functions, we need the first of these so that the function stays monotone increasing, and the second condition ensures that the function stays averaging.

Weighted Arithmetic Mean

A weighted version of the arithmetic mean looks like this:

WAMw(x)=∑i=1nwixi=w1x1+w2x2+⋯+wnxn{"version":"1.1","math":"\\ WAM_{\mathbf w}(\mathbf x) = \sum\limits_{i=1}^n w_i x_i = w_1x_1 + w_2x_2 + \cdots + w_nx_n \\"}

Example

Evaluate the weighted arithmetic mean when w=(0.5,0.2,0.3){"version":"1.1","math":"\(\mathbf w = (0.5, 0.2, 0.3)\)"} and the input x=(0.6,0.7,0.1){"version":"1.1","math":"\(\mathbf x = (0.6, 0.7, 0.1)\)"}.

WAM=(0.5×0.6)+(0.2×0.7)+(0.3×0.1)=0.47{"version":"1.1","math":"\\ WAM = (0.5 \times 0.6) + (0.2 \times 0.7) + (0.3 \times 0.1) = 0.47 \\"}

If we increase x1{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x_1\)"} by 0.1 then the output will go up to 0.52{"version":"1.1","math":"\(0.52\)"}, however if we increase x2{"version":"1.1","math":"\(x_2\)"} by 0.1 then the output only goes up to 0.49{"version":"1.1","math":"\(0.49\)"}.

Interpreting Weights

The usual interpretation of weights is the importance of that variable. However it can refer to other concepts too.

  • In calculated average study scores in university, the weights are often only increased if a unit is a 2-credit point unit. However for some scholarship applications, they might giver third year units higher priority than 2nd or first year units by making these weights higher.
  • Weights could also be used if inputs are reflective of a proportion of the population involved in a decision process. For example, if we were doing peer evaluation and 5 people recommended a score of 7{"version":"1.1","math":"\(7\)"}, 2{"version":"1.1","math":"\(2\)"} people recommended a score of 6{"version":"1.1","math":"\(6\)"} and 3{"version":"1.1","math":"\(3\)"} people recommended a score of 5{"version":"1.1","math":"\(5\)"}, then rather than aggregate AM(7,7,7,7,7,6,6,5,5,5){"version":"1.1","math":"\(AM(7,7,7,7,7,6,6,5,5,5)\)"} we could calculate a weighted arithmetic mean of x=⟨7,6,5⟩{"version":"1.1","math":"\(\mathbf x = \langle 7,6,5\rangle\)"} with weights  w=⟨0.5,0.2,0.3⟩{"version":"1.1","math":"\(\mathbf w = \langle 0.5, 0.2, 0.3\rangle\)"}.
